Can a Landlord Deduct Security Deposit for Normal Wear and Tear in India?

By RentyBase May 2025 9 min read

You've been a good tenant. Paid rent on time for two years. Never had a major dispute. Now you're moving out, and your landlord is holding ₹40,000 of your deposit "for repairs" — including repainting a wall that was already discoloured when you moved in and replacing tiles that were cracked before you signed the agreement.

This happens every day across India. And most tenants don't know they have rights — or how to exercise them.

The core principle

In India, as in most countries, a landlord cannot deduct security deposit for normal wear and tear resulting from ordinary use. They can deduct for actual damage you caused beyond what's expected from everyday living. The challenge is proving what was pre-existing.

What Is "Normal Wear and Tear"?

Normal wear and tear refers to the gradual deterioration of a property from everyday use — things that happen naturally over time regardless of how careful a tenant is. It's the opposite of negligence or deliberate damage.

✓ Normal Wear and Tear (Not Chargeable)
  • Faded or chalky paint after 2+ years
  • Minor scuffs or marks on walls from furniture
  • Small nail holes from hanging pictures
  • Worn or faded carpet from walking on it
  • Loose door or window hinges from normal use
  • Worn finish on wooden floors
  • Yellowing of grout over time
  • Faded or worn curtains/blinds
  • Minor scratches on hard floor surfaces
✗ Actual Damage (Chargeable)
  • Holes in walls beyond small nail holes
  • Burns on carpet, countertops, or floors
  • Broken tiles, fixtures, or glass
  • Stains that can't be removed with normal cleaning
  • Broken doors, windows, or locks
  • Pet damage (scratches, odour, stains)
  • Mould caused by tenant negligence (e.g. blocking ventilation)
  • Missing or broken appliances/fittings
  • Damage from modifications without permission

What Indian Law Says

India doesn't have a single central law governing security deposits and wear and tear — it's governed by a combination of state rent control acts, the Transfer of Property Act, and the terms of your rental agreement.

State Rent Control Acts

Most major states — Maharashtra (Maharashtra Rent Control Act 1999), Karnataka (Karnataka Rent Act 2001), Tamil Nadu (Tamil Nadu Buildings (Lease and Rent Control) Act), Delhi (Delhi Rent Control Act) — recognize the distinction between wear and tear and actual damage. The general principle across these acts: tenants are responsible for damage they cause, not for the natural deterioration of the property.

The Model Tenancy Act, 2021

The central government's Model Tenancy Act 2021 — which states are encouraged to adopt — specifically addresses deposits. Under this act:

  • Security deposit for residential property is capped at 2 months' rent
  • The landlord must return the deposit within one month of vacating, minus legitimate deductions
  • Deductions must be made for actual losses and damage beyond normal wear and tear
  • The tenant can approach a Rent Authority for disputes

As of 2025, Maharashtra, Uttar Pradesh, Andhra Pradesh, and a few other states have adopted versions of this act. Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, and others still operate under older acts.

The enforcement gap

The law may be on your side, but enforcement requires filing a complaint with a Rent Authority or small claims court. This takes time and effort that many tenants don't pursue, which is why landlords get away with wrongful deductions. The practical solution is to prevent the dispute with documentation — not fight it in court.

How to Protect Your Security Deposit Before You Move Out

1. Take Move-In Photos (The Most Important Step)

On the day you move in — before you put any furniture or belongings inside — photograph every single room. Cover:

  • Every wall, ceiling, and floor
  • Kitchen: countertops, tiles, inside cabinets, appliances
  • Bathrooms: tiles, fixtures, toilet, shower
  • All doors, windows, locks, and hinges
  • Balconies and exterior-facing surfaces

Make sure photos are timestamped (most smartphones timestamp automatically in the EXIF data). The timestamp proves the photos were taken on move-in day, not fabricated later. Send these photos to your landlord via WhatsApp or email immediately — this creates a shared record.

2. Document Pre-Existing Damage in Writing

If you notice any damage on move-in day — a crack in the bathroom tile, a stain on the wall, a broken window latch — write it down and get the landlord to acknowledge it in writing (WhatsApp message works, email is better). This prevents them from claiming it was your damage at move-out.

3. Get a Move-Out Inspection Done Together

When you're vacating, do a walkthrough of the property together with your landlord. Ask for a written inspection report. If they identify damage, get them to specify each item and the estimated cost. Don't agree to a blanket deduction — insist on itemized billing.

4. Keep All Rent Receipts

A landlord who wants to dispute your deposit sometimes claims unpaid rent as a reason to withhold funds. Keep every receipt for rent paid. On RentyBase, all payment records are stored permanently — both parties can see them.

What to Do If Your Landlord Wrongfully Withholds Deposit

If your landlord withholds your deposit without justification, or deducts for normal wear and tear, you have these options:

  1. Send a legal notice. A formal legal notice (drafted by a lawyer, sent by registered post) often resolves the dispute — landlords realize you're serious and that a court battle isn't worth ₹15,000 in deductions. Legal notice fees are typically ₹1,000–₹3,000.
  2. File with Rent Authority. Under the Model Tenancy Act (in states that have adopted it), you can file a complaint with the Rent Authority. The process is faster and cheaper than civil court.
  3. File in Consumer Court. Security deposit disputes can be brought to District Consumer Disputes Redressal Forum as a service deficiency. This is especially effective when the landlord is a builder or large property company.
  4. Small Claims / Civil Court. For deposits above ₹50,000, civil court may be necessary. Time-consuming but winnable with good documentation.
Your strongest evidence

Timestamped move-in photos + written acknowledgment of pre-existing damage + all rent paid receipts. With these three things, a landlord attempting a wrongful deduction will face an uphill battle in any forum.

Deposit Deductions: A Landlord Checklist

(For landlords reading this: here's what you can legitimately deduct)

  • Damage beyond normal wear and tear (with before/after photos)
  • Professional cleaning if the tenant left the property in unacceptable condition
  • Unpaid rent or utility bills (with documentation)
  • Costs to repair or replace items specifically damaged by the tenant
  • Penalties specified in the rental agreement that the tenant violated

What you cannot deduct: normal painting between tenancies, cleaning of naturally accumulated dust/grime, replacement of ageing fixtures or appliances that have reached end of life, or repairs for damage that pre-existed the tenancy.
